I went to my first live baseball game of the season last weekend and ended up spending most of it watching the starting pitcher instead of the actual play.

There's something almost meditative about a pitcher's routine between deliveries. The step back, the grip adjustment, the long exhale before setting. Hitters have maybe four seconds to read what's coming, but the pitcher has been building to that single motion for the entire week leading up to the start. All that preparation compressed into one release point.

The thing that caught my attention was how a fastball and a changeup can look completely identical out of the hand. The arm speed, the shoulder turn, the wrist position — all the same. The only difference is in the grip, hidden inside the glove until the ball is already gone. Deception at that level feels less like athleticism and more like sleight of hand.

By the fifth inning the starter's velocity had dropped about two miles per hour. Nobody in the stands seemed to notice, but the catcher started setting up slightly more inside, nudging hitters off their preferred contact zone. That small adjustment probably saved the game more than any single pitch.

Left before the ninth. Felt a little guilty, but honestly the pitcher's duel in the middle innings was the whole thing for me.
